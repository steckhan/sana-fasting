# Blood Pressure During Fasting — Interpretation Guide

_Example context: baseline 140/95 (Stage 1 hypertension), Withings cuff._
_Last updated: 2026-02-26._

---

## Client Baseline & Target

| Metric | Baseline | Expected During Fast | Alert Zone |
|--------|----------|---------------------|------------|
| Systolic | 140 mmHg | 125–135 (drop is good) | <90 or >160 |
| Diastolic | 95 mmHg | 80–90 (drop is good) | <60 or >105 |
| Heart Rate | ~77 bpm | 65–80 (may slow slightly) | <50 or >100 at rest |

**The drop in BP during therapeutic fasting is NOT a problem — it's the point.**
For someone at 140/95, a 5–10 mmHg reduction is a clinically meaningful win.

---

## What Happens to BP During Extended Fasting

### Days 1–2: Slight fluctuation, may rise briefly
- Norepinephrine and cortisol rise initially → can bump BP slightly
- Body is shedding water and sodium → may see a 2–4 mmHg dip

### Day 3–4: Stabilization and drop
- Insulin drops → kidneys release sodium → BP falls
- Ketone production → vasodilatory effect
- Typical drop from baseline: 5–10 mmHg systolic

### Day 5: Lowest point (for most people)
- Maximum fasting-induced BP reduction
- Monitor closely if below 120/75

---

## Understanding the Readings

### Two readings close together (like 134/92 and 124/89 at 07:12 and 07:14)
- This is **white coat / measurement anxiety** effect — normal
- **Use the second reading** (lower, more accurate after initial relaxation)
- Or average them; discard outliers >10 mmHg apart

### Morning vs Evening Variance
- Normal diurnal variation: systolic 5–15 mmHg higher in evening
- **Morning surge** (highest reading 6–10am): expected, not alarming
- **Evening dip**: healthy sign of a well-functioning circadian rhythm
- Concern: if evening is HIGHER than morning by >10 mmHg → non-dipping pattern (cardiovascular risk marker)

### Example data:
| Time | Sys | Dia | HR | Context |
|------|-----|-----|----|---------|
| 07:12 | 134 | 92 | 77 | Pre-fast baseline |
| 07:14 | 124 | 89 | 78 | ✅ Second reading (more accurate) |
| 21:13 | 138 | 100 | 77 | Evening — elevated diastolic ⚠️ |

> Evening diastolic of 100 is above target — worth monitoring. Could be stress, salt, or normal variance. Not alarming in isolation, but track daily during the fast.

## Orthostatic Hypotension — The Key Risk During Fasting

**Definition:** Drop of ≥20 mmHg systolic (or ≥10 mmHg diastolic) within 3 minutes of standing.

**Why it happens during fasting:**
- Dehydration reduces blood volume
- Electrolyte depletion (sodium/potassium) reduces vascular tone
- Extended fast → autonomic nervous system fatigue
- Heat, hot baths, sudden movement → amplifies risk

**How to test:** Take BP lying down, then immediately upon standing. A 20+ point drop = orthostatic hypotension.

**Prevention:**
- Drink 2–3L water/day minimum
- Add pinch of sea salt to water if dizzy
- Rise slowly (sit first, pause, then stand)
- Epsom salt baths: keep cool, not too long

---

## Red Flags — Break the Fast / Contact Doctor

🔴 **Immediate action needed:**
- Systolic < 90 mmHg
- Fainting or near-fainting
- Heart palpitations (irregular, fast, or pounding)
- Chest pain or pressure
- Extreme confusion, slurred speech
- Uncontrolled shaking

🟡 **Alert me (coach) right away:**
- Systolic drops > 20 mmHg from previous reading with dizziness
- Diastolic consistently > 105 during fast
- Diastolic < 60
- HR consistently > 95 at rest
- HR consistently < 55 at rest (unless very fit)
- Morning readings trending UPWARD during fast (shouldn't happen)

🟢 **Normal — keep going:**
- BP gradually decreasing from baseline (even significantly)
- Mild dizziness when standing quickly → drink water + salt
- HR slightly lower than usual (fasting bradycardia — common, benign)

---

## Medications & Fasting Interaction

**Critical question for the client:** Are you on any antihypertensive medications?
- Beta-blockers during fasting → can cause exaggerated BP drops
- ACE inhibitors + fasting → BP may go too low
- Diuretics + fasting → dehydration risk doubled
- **Action: Discuss medication management during fast with your doctor before Day 1**

## BP Interpretation Algorithm (for daily coach check-ins)

```
Morning reading:
  SBP < 90 → RED: stop fast, assess
  SBP 90–105 → YELLOW: hydrate, rest, recheck in 1h
  SBP 106–125 → GREEN: trending well (good drop from 140 baseline)
  SBP 126–140 → GREEN: typical early-fast range
  SBP > 140 → Note: unexpected; check stress, sleep, hydration
  SBP > 160 → YELLOW: unusual during fast; contact doctor

Evening reading:
  Compare to morning: evening should be similar or slightly higher (5-10 mmHg)
  Evening DBP > 100 consistently → worth flagging to doctor
```

## Smart Tracking Pattern During Fast

**Ideal Withings routine:**
1. Morning reading: upon waking, before oats, after 5 min sitting quietly
2. Evening reading: ~9pm, same conditions
3. If dizzy anytime: immediate reading (document the symptom + reading together)
